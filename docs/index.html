Who wants to be a Belyionaire?

Below are four dessins d'enfants (children's drawings), which are a certain kind of bicolored graph. Each one represents a different Belyi map—three-pointed branched covers of the Riemann sphere. Three of them are related by symmetries of algebraic numbers, while one of them stands alone. Can you spot the odd one out?

In this case, one map stands alone because we can describe it with rational numbers, which have no symmetries that do anything. To describe the other three maps, we also need a root of the polynomial \(x^3 - x^2 + 2x - 38\). These three maps, and the numbers that describe them, are related by the symmetries that exchange the roots of the polynomial.

What is a Belyi map?

A Belyi map is a holomorphic map \(\varphi: X \to \widehat{\mathbb{C}}\) from a Riemann surface to the Riemann sphere whose critical points each map to \(0, 1\), or \(\infty\). Equivalently, a Belyi map \(\varphi: X \to \mathbb{P}^1\) is a morphism of algebraic curves over \(\mathbb{C}\) that is unramified outside of \(\{0, 1, \infty\}\).

Why are they interesting?

In 1979, G. V. Belyi proved that an algebraic curve over \(\mathbb{C}\) can be defined over \(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}\) if and only if it admits a Belyi map. In this case, the Belyi map can also be defined over \(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}\).

What is a dessin d'enfant?

A dessin d'enfant (child's drawing) is a finite, connected graph \(D\) embedded in an oriented compact topological surface \(X\) whose vertices are bicolored, such that its complement \(X \setminus D\) is the union of finitely many topological discs.

Why are they interesting?

In 1984, Grothendieck gave a bijection between Belyi maps and dessins d'enfants. One direction is easy to describe: we first realize the unit interval \(I\) as a dessin on \(\mathbb{P}^1\) by putting a white vertex at \(0\), a black vertex at \(1\), and connecting them with an edge along the real axis. Given a Belyi map \(\varphi: X \to \mathbb{P}^1\), we obtain a dessin \(D\) on \(X\) by pulling back \(I\) under \(\varphi\), so \(D = \varphi^{-1}(I)\).

Given a Belyi map \(\varphi: X \to \mathbb{P}^1\), both \(X\) and \(\varphi\) can be defined by equations with coefficients in \(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}\). Thus we get an action of the absolute Galois group \(\operatorname{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})\) on the set of Belyi maps: an element \(\sigma \in \operatorname{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})\) acts on \(\varphi\) by acting on its coefficients.

Since Belyi maps and dessins are in bijection, there is an induced action of \(\operatorname{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})\) on the set of dessins. Grothendieck wondered if there was a way to understand the action \(\operatorname{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})\) on Belyi maps through this induced action on these children's drawings. In particular, can one tell if two dessins are Galois conjugate simply by studying their combinatorial or topological features? This would provide a very concrete way of understanding the important and mysterious group \(\operatorname{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})\)!

In this game, we present you with a collection of dessins, and ask you to try group them into Galois orbits, i.e., place all dessins that are Galois conjugate to each other in the corresponding boxes. Can you find some distinguishing features that tell you that two dessins are Galois conjugate? This is no small feat, so don't be discouraged if you get stumped!
